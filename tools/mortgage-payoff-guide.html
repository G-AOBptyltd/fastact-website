Things to Know

What Do Financially Savvy Mortgage Holders Know?

General concepts and publicly available strategies. Think of these as good questions to ask your broker, adviser or tax accountant — not recommendations to act.

ℹ️General educational information based on ATO published rulings, ACCC reports and published lender practices. Not recommendations. Everyone's situation is different.
📅
How Fortnightly Repayments Actually Work
A calendar year has 26 fortnights — not 24. If your fortnightly payment is exactly half your monthly payment, you make the equivalent of 13 monthly payments per year. On a $600K loan at 6.3%/30yr, that can reduce the loan by roughly 4–5 years. Not all lenders calculate this way — ask your lender or broker.
📊 Potentially significant over time
🏦
How Offset Accounts Reduce Interest
Lenders calculate interest daily on your loan balance minus what's in your offset account. Parking your salary in the offset — even briefly — reduces the average daily balance interest is calculated on. Whether an offset is right for you depends on your loan's fees and habits.
💡 Worth understanding when choosing a loan
💳
The Credit Card Float Concept
Some borrowers pay monthly expenses on a 55-day interest-free card while salary sits in the offset longer. Only works if you pay the card in full every month. Credit card interest (18–22%) eliminates any benefit immediately. Not suitable for everyone.
⚡ Modest benefit — discipline required
📊
The Documented "Loyalty Gap"
The ACCC Home Loan Price Inquiry (November 2020) found existing borrowers paying materially more than new customers — 0.29% for loans under 1 year old to 1.04% for loans over 10 years. Some borrowers address this through a broker-led rate review or refinancing.
📞 Worth raising with your broker
🔄
Understanding Debt Recycling
Converting non-deductible PPOR debt to deductible investment debt. The ATO accepts this under TR 2000/2 when correctly structured with separate loan splits. Carries Part IVA risk if incorrectly implemented. Requires a registered tax agent and financial adviser.
⚖️ Legal but requires professional advice
⚠️
The Redraw vs Offset Tax Distinction
ATO TR 2000/2 treats redraw withdrawals as new borrowings — private use permanently reduces deductibility on an investment loan. Offset withdrawals have no such consequence. One of the most commonly misunderstood areas of property tax.
🛡️ Critical before renting a property
📈
Offset vs Term Deposit — After-Tax
Money in your offset earns the equivalent of your home loan rate — tax-free. A term deposit earns taxable interest. At 39% marginal rate, a 5.0% term deposit nets ~3.05% after tax; a 6.3% offset is worth ~6.3% after tax. Discuss with a financial adviser.
💡 Important for higher-income earners
🎯
How the APRA Buffer Works
APRA requires banks to assess repayability at your actual rate plus 3 percentage points. At 6.3%, you're assessed at 9.3% — significantly reducing borrowing capacity. APRA has provisions for a lower buffer in some refinancing situations where current repayments demonstrate serviceability.
📋 Useful to know when refinancing
📐
Multiple Offset Accounts
Several lenders allow multiple offset accounts linked to one loan — CBA allows up to 99, NAB and Westpac expanded to 10 in early 2025. Some borrowers use named "buckets" for different goals. Not all lenders offer this — a feature to compare when choosing a loan.
💡 A feature to compare
🔍
Comparison Rate vs Interest Rate
ASIC requires lenders to display a comparison rate alongside advertised rates — it incorporates most fees into a standardised calculation ($150K/25yr). Far better than headline rates alone. Always ask for and compare comparison rates.
📋 Always compare comparison rates
🏗️
Rate Lock for Fixed Loans
When applying for a fixed-rate loan, a 4–8 week settlement gap means rates can change. A "rate lock" (~0.15% of loan) guarantees the rate applied for. With the RBA raising rates three times in the first five months of 2026, some borrowers found rate locks worthwhile.
📊 Worth discussing in volatile markets
🏛️
First Home Super Saver Scheme
Eligible first home buyers can make voluntary super contributions of up to $15,000/year ($50,000 lifetime, $100,000 for couples) and withdraw them for a deposit. Contributions taxed at 15% inside super rather than your marginal rate (up to 47%). Eligibility rules are strict — confirm with your super fund and a financial adviser.
🏠 Significant for first home buyers
